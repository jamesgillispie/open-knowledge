# Getty PST 2030 Platform Proposal

AREA 17's final-round proposal for a unified digital platform for [Getty's Pacific Standard Time (PST 2030)](/clients/getty-pst/overview.md) initiative — a grant program funding ~100 LA museums, galleries, and universities to mount exhibitions on California art. Now in its fourth, ~25-year-running iteration, Getty wants to scale PST into a persistent brand and digital home that competes with biennials like Frieze.

## Status

Made what appears to be the **final round**. Proposal document due **June 30, 2026**; presentation **July 6**. An intro call with Getty leadership (James, [Mark](/people/mark.md), [Mike](/people/mike.md)) ran June 22 to vet early structure thinking.

## Scope

Three core workstreams plus supporting infrastructure:

- **Public-facing marketing site** — the visitor experience.
- **Partner portal** — the most technically complex piece: ~100 grantee entities each need logins, intake forms, tracking, and content pipelines (descriptions, images, titles) that surface on the marketing site. Previously run on Google Forms, which became unmanageable. Must be simple enough that grantees "just log in" with minimal support. Getty manages the roster and all grant-making/correspondence on getty.edu; AREA 17 does not interface with individual institutions.
- **Narrative archive** — storytelling/interactive (not static), able to grow dynamically post-2030.
- **CMS + CRM as supporting infrastructure** — the CMS acts as an orchestration layer tying content uploads, potential CRM feeds, and public output together; data packets from grantees could feed getty.edu and return metrics. CRM scope is still undefined (candidates include Salesforce/HubSpot; could be as simple as a newsletter tool).

Budget: ~$125K covering everything through end of 2030; hosting separate; post-2030 maintenance is a new budget.

## Key decision

**Frame the proposal as one unified product** — a single platform spanning visitor, partner, and archive experiences — rather than five modular workstreams. The archive is positioned as a dotted-line / flexible item, CMS + CRM as supporting infrastructure, with named technology recommendations (Salesforce / HubSpot) provided now rather than deferred. The client wants one unified product, a concrete timeline, and a full personalization CRM. This addresses recurring tension around the [sprint vs. retainer model](/concepts/sprint-vs-retainer.md) for complex multi-experience platforms.

Sequencing remains open across three options: traditional (RFP-aligned), parallel (marketing site + partner portal together, archive later), or archive-first (launch the archive alongside the marketing site for a press moment).

## Case studies cited

CES (event cadence), Lucas (LA launch + new visual identity), a small DEA project (archive), and a fourth TBD. Nasim (Nascent) is a strong candidate for demonstrating API orchestration at scale — it unified multiple APIs and endpoints via Twill into a single hub without disrupting legacy systems. Cartier also floated.
