# The Rubber Duck and the Night

There's a moment in the studio — usually late, usually alone — when you put a mark on a canvas and walk away. Not because you're done. Because you can't see it yet.

The mark sits there overnight. Paint dries. Light changes. You come back the next morning and something's shifted. Not the painting — you. The distance gave you what proximity couldn't: clarity about what that mark actually means, where it belongs, whether it stays or gets buried under the next layer.

Painters have always known this. Time is the silent collaborator. You make a move, you step away, you return with different eyes. The work tells you things it couldn't say while you were standing over it.

I've started to notice the same rhythm somewhere else entirely.

## The programmer's rubber duck

In software engineering, there's an old practice called rubber duck debugging. You explain your problem out loud — to a rubber duck on your desk, to a colleague, to anyone — and in the act of articulating it, you find the answer. The duck doesn't solve anything. The act of externalizing does.

It works because thinking isn't just internal. When you push an idea out of your head and into language, you force structure onto it. You hear the gaps. You catch the assumptions you were skating past. The rubber duck is a mirror that makes you see your own reasoning clearly.

For decades, the rubber duck was a person, a toy, a blank wall you talked at. Now it's a machine.

## AI as the new rubber duck

When I use AI to think through a problem, I'm not asking it for the answer. I'm using it the way a programmer uses the duck — as an externalization surface. I describe what I'm thinking. The AI reflects it back, reframed, restructured, sometimes wrong in useful ways. I see my own ideas more clearly because they've been through a second rendering.

But here's where it gets interesting. You can use AI to prompt AI.

First pass: dump your raw thinking into a model. Get a draft, a structure, a restatement. Don't judge it. Let it sit.

Second pass: feed that output into another prompt — or the same model with different instructions. Ask it to critique, reframe, find the weak points. Now you've got two external perspectives on your own idea, neither of which is yours.

Third pass: you. The human. Reading both outputs with the one thing the machines don't have — taste. Context. The feeling in your gut that says this part is right and that part is performing.

This is rubber duck debugging scaled up. You're not explaining to a duck. You're running your thinking through multiple lenses, then applying judgment to the composite.

## But the real trick is time

Here's what most people skip. They run all three passes in ten minutes and ship whatever comes out.

That's like putting a mark on the canvas and painting over it immediately. You never let the work breathe. You never let distance do its job.

The painter's rhythm matters here. Make the mark. Walk away. Come back tomorrow. See what survived the night.

When I draft something with AI, I don't finish it the same session. I let it sit. I come back hours later — sometimes a day later — and read it cold. The parts that were exciting in the moment look different now. Some hold up. Some were the machine performing confidence, not meaning. Some were my own assumptions dressed up in better prose.

Time is the third party. It's the collaborator you can't automate. It strips away the momentum of creation and leaves you with just the work.

In painting, this is literal. You put paint down. The light in your studio at 2 AM is different from the light at 10 AM. The colors shift. The relationships between marks change. What felt resolved at night looks unfinished in the morning, or vice versa. The painting is the same — your perception isn't. And perception is what matters, because perception is what your viewer will bring.

The same applies to any creative work run through AI. The draft doesn't change overnight. But you do. And that change — that recalibration of your own judgment — is the most important part of the process.

## Separation of concerns

In engineering, we talk about separation of concerns — keeping different responsibilities in different places so each one can be understood, maintained, and improved independently. Don't mix your data logic with your display logic. Don't tangle your business rules with your infrastructure.

This is the same principle applied to thinking.

When you use AI to externalize your ideas, you're separating the concern of generation from the concern of evaluation. When you let time pass before revisiting, you're separating the concern of creation from the concern of judgment. When you run a second AI pass to critique the first, you're separating the concern of articulation from the concern of rigor.

Painters do this instinctively. The mark-making is one mode. The stepping back is another. The morning review is a third. Each mode has its own job. Mixing them — trying to judge while you're creating, trying to create while you're judging — produces mediocre work in both directions.

AI gives us new ways to formalize this separation. But the principle is ancient. The studio knew it first.

## What the generalist sees

The people who use AI well aren't the ones who prompt fastest or chain the most tools. They're the ones who understand this rhythm — externalize, separate, let time work, then apply judgment.

They've seen it before. In a different medium, under different conditions, with different tools. But the pattern is the same.

A painter who learned to trust the overnight gap applies that patience to an AI-assisted writing workflow. A musician who learned to record a take and listen back the next day applies that discipline to product decisions. An engineer who learned to separate concerns in code applies that architecture to how they think.

This is the generalist's advantage again. Not knowing more tools. Knowing the deeper patterns that make any tool useful.

The rubber duck doesn't care what domain you're in. Time doesn't either. And the discipline to use both — to externalize, to pause, to return with fresh eyes and honest judgment — that's not a skill you learn from a tutorial.

It's a skill you learn from going deep. Somewhere. Anywhere. Then carrying it forward.
