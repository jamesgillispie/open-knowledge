# Rise of the Generalist

I know what mastery feels like. That's the problem.

I spent years training as a painter. MFA, studio practice, the whole thing. I learned what it means to go deep — to put in the hours until your hands know something your mind can't quite articulate. That kind of depth becomes part of you.

Then I moved into design and technology. And suddenly I was surrounded by people who had that same depth, but in code, in design, in product thinking. They'd spent a decade going down while I'd been somewhere else entirely.

I couldn't match them. Not because I was lost or wasting time — I knew exactly what I'd been doing. But because I knew what real depth felt like, I could feel the gap.

That's what made it uncomfortable. I understood the distance between competence and mastery, and I was on the wrong side of it.

This felt like a weakness for a long time. It wasn't.

## Depth in one place changes how you see everything else

Here's what I've come to understand: having gone deep in something — anything — trains you in ways that transfer.

Painting taught me that creation is an action, not a passive act — inspiration doesn't strike while you're waiting for it, it happens when you're working. That the foundation matters as much as the work itself: do the hard things first, do them incredibly well, and the rest gets easier. That preparing the perfect surface to paint on is as important as the painting itself.

In technology, I've found that accessibility and design systems are the underlying structure — much like a stretcher and primed canvas. This is true for painting, for design, engineering and organizations.

The specialists around me had depth I couldn't match in their domain. But I had a different kind of training. I knew what mastery required — the hours, the practice, the taste you develop only through repetition. I could recognize quality in other fields, even when I couldn't perform it myself.

That recognition turns out to be its own skill.

## The generalist isn't someone who skipped depth

This is the part most people get wrong. The generalists I know — the ones who are actually good — aren't dabblers. They're people who went deep somewhere, then carried that depth across boundaries.

The discipline transfers. The understanding of what "good" means transfers. The patience for craft transfers.

You don't become a useful generalist by learning a little about a lot. You become one by learning what depth actually costs, then applying that standard everywhere you go. By understanding that foundation matters, that you have to do the hard things first, that what you build on shapes everything that follows.

When I moved into technology, I didn't abandon what painting taught me. I just started using it in a new context. How do you compose a system? How do you balance the parts and the whole? When is something done? When have you prepared the surface well enough to begin?

These are the same questions. The medium changed. The practice didn't.

## The machines go deep now

Here's what shifted. We're in a moment where AI can do the first 80% of almost any specialized task. It's read everything. Trained on everything. It can generate code, essays, designs, strategies — passable versions, instantly.

Depth is being commoditized.

What's left is the 20% that requires judgment. Taste. Integration. Knowing which questions to ask. Recognizing when the marketing problem is actually a design problem is actually a people problem. Sensing when the foundation isn't right, even if you can't articulate why.

This is where generalists live. Not because we're smarter, but because we've been practicing this all along — working across boundaries, translating between disciplines, holding the whole composition while others work the details.

The specialists spent a decade going deep. The machines went deeper. But the machines don't know what to want. They can't feel when something's off. They can't carry an understanding of mastery from one domain to another. They skip the foundation and go straight to the surface — and if you've ever painted, you know how that ends.

That's the generalist's edge. Not knowing more — but knowing across.

## The rise

I'm not arguing everyone should become a generalist. Specialists are essential. The world needs people who go all the way down.

But if you've felt exposed — if you've known the weight of real depth in one place, then had to work in spaces where you couldn't match it — you might be more prepared for this moment than you think.

The rise of the generalist isn't about replacing expertise. It's about what happens when expertise becomes accessible. When anyone can generate a first draft, the last 20% becomes everything.

Taste. Judgment. Integration. The ability to hold the whole while everyone else is zoomed in. The discipline to do the hard things first. The understanding that creation is an action — you have to show up and work before the inspiration arrives.

We've been training for this all along. We just didn't know it.
